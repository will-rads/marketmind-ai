# MarketMind

**Review Intelligence for Marketing Generation**

A review-intelligence system that surfaces review data from similar products and turns real customer feedback into evidence-backed marketing strategy and campaign generation. A trained sentiment classifier organizes reviews at scale, an algorithmic ranking layer surfaces the most-reviewed products in each category with rich theme intelligence, and an agentic Gemini pipeline generates grounded ad copy and promotional images from curated review excerpts.

## Why This Exists

People launching product-based businesses online — whether dropshipping, private label, or small e-commerce brands — face the same problem when it comes to marketing. They pick a product, skim a handful of reviews, and write ad copy based on whatever sounds convincing. The actual patterns buried across thousands of reviews — what buyers consistently love, what frustrates them, what almost made them return it — none of that gets read at scale. There is too much of it, and nobody has time.

MarketMind bridges that gap. A trained model classifies review sentiment at scale — acting as a scalable text-based sentiment approximation that organizes the review corpus into meaningful buckets. An algorithmic ranking layer uses those buckets to surface the most-reviewed products in each category with rich theme intelligence. Curated review excerpts from each sentiment group give Gemini the real customer language it needs to generate campaign assets that speak to actual strengths and address actual objections, not guesswork.

## Architecture

### Stage 1 — Trained Model: 3-Class Sentiment Classifier (Preprocessing)

**Task:** Classify product reviews into three sentiment classes by collapsing star ratings:
- **Positive** (4-5 stars)
- **Neutral** (3 stars)
- **Negative** (1-2 stars)

Star ratings are used as labels. This is a known noisy proxy — a 3-star review saying "love the product, terrible shipping" is mixed sentiment labeled as neutral. This limitation is stated honestly and explored in the evaluation. Because the model is trained on star-derived labels, it should be understood as a **scalable text-based sentiment approximation** — it learns to predict star-level sentiment from review text, not to discover sentiment beyond what stars capture.

**What the classifier enables (not what it directly produces):**
- **Product ranking:** Per-product sentiment profiles (% positive, % neutral, % negative) that the algorithmic ranking layer uses to surface review intelligence from similar products
- **Review grouping:** Separating reviews into sentiment pools so the most representative excerpts from each group can be selected and passed to Gemini
- **Scalability:** Processing 50K+ reviews in minutes — something manual reading cannot do

**This stage runs as batch preprocessing, not at demo time.** The trained model classifies all reviews in the dataset. Each product then gets a precomputed sentiment profile that downstream stages use. This keeps the demo fast and the model's work reusable.

**Dataset:** Amazon Reviews 2023 (McAuley Lab, UCSD)
- Source: https://huggingface.co/datasets/McAuley-Lab/Amazon-Reviews-2023
- **10 broad categories:** Beauty & Personal Care, Electronics, Home & Kitchen, Sports & Outdoors, Toys & Games, Pet Supplies, Health & Household, Baby Products, Office Products, Tools & Home Improvement
- **Sampling strategy:** Top-reviewed products per category. For each category, reviews are taken from the most-reviewed products first (not randomly), ensuring dense review coverage per product. This is critical for the ranking layer — products need enough reviews to build reliable sentiment profiles.
- Equal representation: 5K reviews per category (50K MVP) / 10K per category (100K final)
- Sample size: **50K reviews for MVP, 100K for final model** — mixed across all categories
- Labels: Star ratings (1-5) are pre-existing in the dataset — collapsed to 3 classes
- **Class imbalance (confirmed by EDA):** Positive 84.5% (42,253), negative 9.1% (4,526), neutral 6.4% (3,210) out of 49,989 reviews. Imbalance ratio ~13:1. Class weighting will be applied during training to prevent the model from defaulting to "positive" for everything. Both TF-IDF + LR (via `class_weight='balanced'`) and DistilBERT (via weighted loss function) will use this.
- **Product coverage (confirmed by EDA):** 4,173 unique products. Mean 12.0 reviews/product, median 7.0, max 1,518. 1,357 products with 10+ reviews, covering 32,247 reviews (64.5% of data). This density makes the ranking layer viable.

**Models:**

| Model | Role | Training Time (Colab T4) |
|-------|------|--------------------------|
| TF-IDF + Logistic Regression | Baseline — fast, interpretable | Under 2 minutes |
| DistilBERT (fine-tuned) | Main model — stronger on nuance | 20–40 min (50K) / 40–90 min (100K) |

**Fine-tuning approach:**
- DistilBERT sourced pretrained from Hugging Face (`distilbert-base-uncased`)
- Replace classification head for 3-class output
- Fine-tune 3–5 epochs, batch size 16–32, learning rate ~2e-5
- Early debugging runs: 1 epoch sanity checks (~3-5 min each)
- Expect 3–5 experimental runs total (learning rate, class weighting, epoch count)

**Colab free-tier session management:**
- Sessions last ~4–6 hours before disconnection
- Longest single training run is ~90 minutes — well within session limits
- Save checkpoints after each epoch
- Store dataset on Google Drive — no re-downloading per session
- Use CPU sessions for data exploration; reserve GPU sessions for training

### Stage 2 — Product Ranking Layer (Algorithmic, Not a Trained Model)

**Goal:** When the user enters campaign constraints, surface review intelligence from similar products — based on the sentiment profiles computed in Stage 1. The user picks the closest match as their intelligence source.

**How it works:**
1. Each product in the dataset has a precomputed sentiment profile from the classifier:
   - % positive / % neutral / % negative reviews
   - Total review volume
   - Recurring strengths, complaints, and friction points (from theme extraction — see below)
2. User enters campaign constraints: category and campaign goal
3. Backend logic ranks products using the sentiment profiles. The ranking criteria adapt to the campaign goal:
   - **"Highlight strengths"** → rank by high positive ratio + high review volume (proven winners with lots of evidence)
   - **"Address objections"** → rank by products with high volume but mixed sentiment (products people buy but complain about — opportunity to reframe)
   - **"Differentiate"** → rank by products where positive reviews mention distinctive attributes (unique strengths the model surfaced)
4. User sees a ranked list and selects the most similar product as their intelligence source

**This is algorithmic ranking, not a second trained model.** The ranking uses the trained classifier's output as its input. Without the classifier, the ranking would rely on raw star averages alone. The classifier adds value by providing a text-based sentiment approximation at scale, giving the ranking layer per-product sentiment distributions rather than single-number averages.

### Stage 3 — Review Selection & Evidence Curation

**Not a second model.** A structured, reproducible step that selects the most representative review excerpts from each sentiment group.

**Role of TF-IDF:** TF-IDF is used as a **selection mechanism**, not as the insight itself. It identifies which terms distinguish each sentiment group and helps surface the reviews that best represent each group. The actual marketing value comes from the **curated review excerpts** — real customer language that Gemini receives as evidence.

**Pipeline:**
1. For the selected product, take all its reviews classified by the trained model
2. Group reviews by predicted class (positive / neutral / negative)
3. Run TF-IDF on each group relative to the others — identifies distinguishing terms per sentiment class
4. Use those terms to select the most representative reviews from each group
5. Curate 3–5 actual review excerpts per sentiment class as evidence

**Output:** A structured insight object per product:
```json
{
  "product_id": "B00XYZ123",
  "sentiment_profile": {"positive": 0.72, "neutral": 0.15, "negative": 0.13},
  "review_count": 1847,
  "positive_excerpts": [
    "This moisturizer lasts all day and doesn't irritate my sensitive skin...",
    "Great value for the price. Gentle formula, no breakouts after two weeks..."
  ],
  "negative_excerpts": [
    "The bottle is tiny for the price. Felt misled by the listing photo...",
    "Irritated my skin within days. Returned immediately..."
  ],
  "neutral_excerpts": [
    "It's okay. Does what it says but I wouldn't repurchase...",
    "Works fine for the price but nothing special compared to drugstore brands..."
  ]
}
```

**What gets passed to Gemini:** The sentiment profile plus the curated excerpts — not vague keyword lists. Gemini reads the actual customer language and generates copy grounded in what real buyers said.

**Limitation:** Neutral excerpts should be treated conservatively. DistilBERT's neutral F1 is 0.384, meaning some reviews in the neutral group may actually be positive or negative reviews that were misclassified. Evidence snippets help users verify the themes are genuine.

### Stage 4 — Agentic Layer: Gemini Campaign Generator

**Goal:** Use the structured review intelligence to generate a 3-stage campaign plan aligned to Meta's campaign objective system — grounded in real buyer language, not generic AI copy.

**Meta-prompt architecture:** This stage is built around a structured meta-prompt engineered specifically around the pipeline's outputs. Gemini receives:
- The product's sentiment profile (% positive / neutral / negative, review count)
- 3–5 curated positive review excerpts (what real buyers praise)
- 3–5 curated negative review excerpts (what real buyers complain about)
- 1–2 neutral excerpts (friction points, treated conservatively)
- The user's campaign brief (goal, audience, tone)
- The user's business model context (website/landing page, click-to-WhatsApp, or app install)
- The user's brand identity (brand name + product/item name) — distinct from the Amazon review-intelligence source
- Optional brand assets: product image, brand-guidelines PDF, brand-context free text, an uploaded or generated brand logo, and optional promo fields (currency / price / sale percentage)

The excerpts are the key input — they give Gemini actual customer language to work with, not vague summaries. This ensures the generated copy references real strengths and addresses real objections.

**User identity vs review-intelligence source.** The prompt opens with an explicit "USER BRAND / PRODUCT IDENTITY" block that names the user's brand and product separately from the Amazon review-intelligence source product. The review source is labeled EVIDENCE ONLY — Gemini is told not to rename the user's product to the source, not to print the source name on the ad, and not to treat the source as the user's brand. This prevents the trained-model evidence pipeline from leaking into the campaign's surface identity.

**3-stage campaign framework:** Instead of generating a single campaign, Gemini produces a 3-stage campaign plan aligned to how Meta's ad platform actually works:
- **Stage 3 is always Sales** (final conversion objective).
- **Stages 1 and 2:** Gemini selects the most suitable objectives from Meta-style options — Awareness, Traffic, Engagement, Leads, or even Sales if two distinct sales-stage treatments make sense. The only hard constraint is that the final stage must be Sales. The choice for earlier stages is informed by the user's campaign goal, product data, and business model context.
- **Business model context matters:** The user selects their business type (website/landing page, click-to-WhatsApp, app install). This affects which objectives Gemini selects for Stages 1–2. For example, a WhatsApp-based business might get Awareness → Engagement → Sales, while a website business might get Awareness → Traffic → Sales.

**Creative volume:** Stage 1 returns 3 creative blocks, Stage 2 returns 3 creative blocks, Stage 3 returns 5 creative blocks — 11 total across the full plan.

**Per-block output:** Each creative block includes:
- Campaign objective and rationale for stage placement
- Suggested audience age range and interests
- Stage budget % + block budget % (dollar amounts derived from the user's total budget)
- Messaging angle (tied back to specific review themes)
- **On-post copy** rendered onto the static image — headline, primary text, CTA
- **Paste-into-Meta copy** for Meta's caption + headline fields (separate strings, distinct wording from the on-post versions, with one relevant emoji and a soft CTA in the caption)
- Design direction, visual concept, and motion direction (motion is only used for the Reel block)
- Description (Meta ad-description field)

**Call architecture:** One Gemini call per stage (3 total), not one monolithic call. Each call is focused and independently retriable. In the demo, Stage 1 generates first; the user clicks Next for Stage 2, then Next for Stage 3 (progressive loading).

**Models used:**
- **Gemini 3.1 Pro** (`gemini-3.1-pro-preview`, thinking=high) — text generation and campaign strategy
- **Gemini Flash** (`gemini-3-flash-preview`) — theme normalization and per-card re-prompting / refinement
- **Nano Banana Pro** (`gemini-3-pro-image-preview`) — ad-ready image generation with on-post text rendered onto a 4:5 portrait Meta feed-ad. Also used for the optional logo generator (flat 2D, square 1:1, white background) and the optional concept-product render.
- **Veo 3.1 Fast** (`veo-3.1-fast-generate-preview`) — Reel/video generation for block 1 of each stage. Vertical 9:16, 1080p, 8 seconds. Visual-only (no on-video text), localhost/demo mode.

Each creative block includes a visual concept. Image generation uses Nano Banana Pro, which renders the on-post copy (headline, primary text, CTA) onto a **4:5 portrait** Meta feed-ad image (blocks 2+). Aspect ratio is enforced both via the SDK's `imageConfig: { aspectRatio: "4:5" }` and via in-prompt rules. Block 1 of every stage is a vertical 9:16 Reel generated by Veo 3.1 Fast with motion constrained to subtle, product-focused ad-like motion (single scene, at most one slow camera move, no cuts, no text overlays, no dialogue).

**Reference priority on the image prompt.** When the user supplies any combination of product image / brand guidelines PDF / brand logo, the image prompt resolves conflicts in a documented order: (1) product image identity wins for product appearance, (2) brand-guidelines PDF wins for palette and typography, (3) uploaded logo wins for the brand mark unless the PDF contains a clearer official logo, (4) user brand-context free text informs tone and voice, (5) the campaign design direction is the lowest priority and never overrides any visual reference. The prompt ends with a visual-reference compliance checklist (same product type, same silhouette, same colors, logo used if supplied, no invented logo if supplied, promo badges only if supplied, 4:5 portrait output) that the model is asked to verify before generating. Honest limit: this tightens compliance, but exact reproduction of small label text and arbitrary fonts inside a logo remains best-effort.

**Promo badges on static images.** When the user fills in the optional currency, price, or sale-percentage fields on the campaign panel, the image prompt instructs Nano Banana Pro to render a separate price-tag pill and/or a separate high-contrast sale badge in addition to the headline / primary text / CTA. Sale percent is normalized to its numeric portion at every boundary so the rendered badge cannot end up as `20%% off`. When neither field is supplied, the prompt explicitly forbids any price tag, deal sticker, percent-off badge, or fake promotional pricing — the model is told to render no badge rather than invent one. Reel blocks remain text-free and never render promo badges.

**Prompt engineering:** The Gemini prompt templates are documented, disclosed, and reproducible. This is a deliberate contribution — showing that structured prompts grounded in trained model output produce better results than generic prompting alone. The 3-stage structure makes this comparison even more pointed: a generic "write an ad" prompt produces a single undifferentiated output, while the structured pipeline produces a staged plan with distinct objectives, audiences, and creative per stage.

**Why Gemini needs the trained model:** Without the classifier, there is no structured way to separate what buyers love from what they hate at scale. The classifier organizes the review corpus into sentiment groups, the selection step curates the most representative excerpts from each group, and Gemini receives those excerpts as grounded evidence. The trained model structures the data. The ranking layer surfaces the most relevant products as intelligence sources. Gemini turns those grounded insights into campaign assets — with Nano Banana Pro generating ad-ready images with text rendered on them.

**Optional brand assets.** Users can optionally provide brand context to further personalize the output. All paths are additive — every field is optional, and the meta-prompt handles their absence explicitly:

- **Product image** — uploaded by the user (their own packaging, a manufacturer mock-up, or a reference from another brand). Treated as the canonical reference for product appearance: silhouette, packaging type, proportions, colors, and visible brand cues.
- **Brand-guidelines PDF** — fonts, palette, official logo lockup, tone doc. Wins for palette and typography; if it contains an official logo, that logo overrides any separately uploaded mark. Exact font rendering is best-effort.
- **Brand logo (uploaded or generated)** — image-only; either uploaded directly via the BrandPanel logo control or produced by the in-app **logo generator** (Nano Banana Pro, 1:1 square, flat 2D on a white background, with geometry presets — Wordmark / Circle badge / Angular mark / Rounded icon — and theme presets — Modern / Outdoor / Luxury / Playful / Rustic / Tech). The accepted logo data URI persists with the draft, attaches to the image-generation call, and is also handed to the campaign-text prompt so visual concepts know to place it in a corner. The same flow is reachable from the brief-page floating button and from inside the concept-product modal.
- **Concept-product flow** — for users without a real product image yet. Generates a square hero render of a notional package, optionally using an uploaded or generated logo so the rendered packaging carries the user's actual mark. Once accepted, the concept image becomes the `productImage` for every downstream call. Surfaced in the UI as a concept render, not production artwork.
- **Brand context (free text)** — describe the look, feel, tone, packaging shape, colors. Used for tone/voice only when the references above are silent; the brand-guidelines PDF takes priority when they conflict.
- **Promo fields (currency / price / sale percentage)** — optional. When set, Gemini renders separate price + sale badges on static images. When blank, no badge is rendered and no fake price is invented.
- **Skip it entirely** — leave everything blank and Gemini works from the review intelligence and campaign brief alone.

This is handled entirely by Gemini's multimodal generation and does not affect the trained model or the core pipeline.

### Stage 5 — Demo Application

**Interface.** Four primary screens — Landing → Brief → Product Intelligence → Campaign Output — plus a personalised Dashboard reachable from the navbar. The three "step" screens are the linear campaign-creation flow; the Landing and Dashboard are session/account surfaces around it.

**Landing:**
- Video background, real logo, editorial typography. Email-only sign-in (pseudonymous, see Persistence section).

**Screen 1 — Campaign Brief (6-step gated flow):**
- **Step 1 — Brand identity (required):** brand name + product / item name. Both fields must be non-empty before any later step unlocks. These two strings anchor the campaign title across `/campaign`, the dashboard, the PDF, and the email subject.
- **Step 2 — Category:** dropdown over the 10 broad categories (Beauty & Personal Care, Electronics, Home & Kitchen, Sports & Outdoors, Toys & Games, Pet Supplies, Health & Household, Baby Products, Office Products, Tools & Home Improvement).
- **Step 3 — Goal:** highlight strengths, address objections, or differentiate.
- **Step 4 — Business model:** website / landing page, click-to-WhatsApp, or app install.
- **Step 5 — Audience + tones (optional):** target audience (free text) + up to 3 brand tones (Warm & Friendly, Bold & Direct, Professional, Playful, Reassuring, Urgent, Luxurious, Educational).
- **Step 6 — Product description (required, ≥3 chars):** short descriptor used by the deterministic product-type filter on Screen 2 (e.g. "men's perfume", "wireless earbuds", "dog dental chews").
- Floating actions: open the Concept-product flow (which can ingest a logo) or open the standalone Logo generator. Brand assets can also be supplied later inside Screen 3.

**Screen 2 — Product Intelligence:**
- Intentionally surfaces Amazon **review-intelligence source** products — these are not the user's product, they are the evidence pool.
- Two-stage backend: a deterministic alias-based product-type filter narrows the category to products that match the user's descriptor, then the goal-specific ranking formula orders the survivors.
- Ranked list of the most-reviewed matches in the selected category.
- Each card shows: Amazon product name, ASIN, review count, average rating, sentiment distribution bar, evidence-strength badge (rich / moderate / limited), theme percentage breakdowns, and sample positive + negative review excerpts.
- The user picks the closest match — its review data becomes the **evidence source** for campaign generation. The user's own brand/product (Step 1) remains the campaign's display identity.

**Screen 3 — Campaign Output:**
- **Title is the user's identity** (brand name / product name from Step 1). The Amazon source product is rendered as a small secondary "Review intelligence · [short Amazon name]" subheader, and the ASIN drops to a tiny mono caption.
- 3-layer progressive UI: Stage 1 → Stage 2 → Stage 3, generated one stage at a time (3 + 3 + 5 = 11 creative blocks total).
- Each block shows: campaign objective, rationale, audience targeting, stage + block budget % with derived dollar amounts, messaging angle, **on-post copy** (headline + primary text + CTA — what gets rendered onto the static image), **paste-into-Meta copy** (Meta primary text / caption + Meta clickable headline — distinct strings, with copy buttons for Meta posting), design direction, visual concept, and motion direction (used only for the Reel block).
- **Static images are 4:5 portrait** (Meta portrait feed-ad). Generated by Nano Banana Pro with the on-post copy rendered onto the image.
- **Block 1 of every stage is a 9:16 Reel** generated by Veo 3.1 Fast — visual-only, no on-video text.
- BrandPanel (right side) controls: product image upload, brand-guidelines PDF upload, brand-context free text, total budget, optional promo fields (currency / price / sale %), and the logo controls (generate or upload). Once accepted, a logo persists with the draft and feeds every downstream image call.
- Per-card refinement (Gemini Flash) and per-card image regeneration. Rotating review-evidence drawers per card. Pipeline-transparency panel disclosing which model produced what.
- PDF export and "Email this report" both use the user's brand/product as the title; review intelligence is surfaced as a separate row.

**Dashboard (`/dashboard`):**
- Reachable from the navbar after sign-in.
- Stats tiles, mix breakdowns (goal / category / business model), completion funnel, recent product-intelligence list, and a "Recent reports" surface.
- Report cards are titled with the user's brand/product (display-name priority chain falls through `campaignProductName → brandName → identifiedProductType → productDescription → review intelligence → ASIN`). When the user filled in brand or product, a small `Review intelligence: <short>` subline sits beneath. Older drafts that have only the Amazon source name keep it as the title without a redundant subline.
- Per-report actions: in-app HTML preview (rebuilt on demand from the saved draft, no PDFs stored on disk), PDF download, and View campaign (Resume).

**Deployment:** Next.js frontend. The persistence layer (SQLite + base64 media in draft rows) is localhost-only — see the Persistence section. Precomputed pipeline outputs (sentiment profiles, theme intelligence, product names) are served as static JSON. Gemini API calls run from serverless-style API routes. The heavy ML work is done offline; the demo serves precomputed intelligence plus live Gemini generation.

### Persistence, Sessions, and Email (localhost)

The webapp now has a light persistence layer so the user can walk away from a half-generated campaign, come back later, and finish it — or revisit previously generated campaigns. This is explicitly a **localhost demo**, not production auth or a hosted service.

- **Drafts:** SQLite (`better-sqlite3`) with a single file at `webapp-final/data/marketmind.db` (gitignored). One table, `drafts`, keyed by a UUID, owned by the session email. Free-form JSON columns store: the brief (including `brandName`, `campaignProductName`, and the optional promo fields `promoCurrency` / `promoPrice` / `promoSalePct`), the selected product's intelligence object, generated stages, generated ad images (base64) and Reel videos (base64), the optional generated brand-guidelines HTML, and the optional accepted brand logo as a data URI under `media.logo`. No DB schema migrations were required for the brand/product/promo/logo additions — they all live inside the existing JSON columns. Drafts auto-save on `/campaign` whenever stages / refinements / media / brand assets / promo fields change, with a "Save draft" button for explicit saves.
- **Session:** An HttpOnly cookie (`mm_session`) holds the user's lowercased email. No password, no verification, no OAuth. The email is a pseudonymous key — anyone with access to this machine and that email can see the drafts tagged to it. This is stated to the user directly on the brief screen.
- **Dashboard (`/dashboard`):** Lists drafts for the current session email. Includes stats tiles (campaigns, total budget, average budget, static creatives, Reels), mix breakdowns (goal / category / business model), a completion funnel, a recent-product-intelligence list, a "Recent reports" surface for completed drafts, and Resume / Delete per draft. **Report cards and the in-app preview modal are titled with the user's brand/product**, with a small "Review intelligence: [short Amazon name]" subline when the user supplied identity (older drafts that predate the brand/product fields keep the Amazon source name as the title). Resume hydrates the campaign context and routes the user back to `/brief` or `/campaign` depending on where they left off.
- **Email report:** `POST /api/send-email` accepts a base64 PDF and uses [Resend](https://resend.com) to deliver it. The subject line and HTML body lead with the user-facing campaign name; review intelligence is surfaced as a separate "Review intelligence source:" row in the body, never in the subject. If `RESEND_API_KEY` is absent, the endpoint returns a clear 503 with a message pointing to `.env.local` — the rest of the app still works.
- **PDF export:** Client-side jsPDF, no server round-trip. Cover page leads with the user-facing campaign title, with a small "REVIEW INTELLIGENCE · [short Amazon name]" line beneath; the brief table renders Brand / Product-or-item / Review-intelligence rows. Static images are embedded at 4:5 portrait (matching the in-app card aspect); Reel placeholders share the same 4:5 box so static and Reel cards stay row-aligned. Text is run through a PDF-only sanitizer that strips emojis, smart quotes, ZWJs, and other Helvetica-incompatible characters, plus collapses Gemini's occasional letter-spaced output ("T a c t i l e   l e a t h e r" → "Tactile leather"). The app UI keeps the original emoji-rich strings for verbatim Meta paste — sanitization is PDF-only.
- **What's not saved with a draft:** product image and brand-guidelines PDF uploads. Base64-storing multi-megabyte file uploads in the draft row would balloon the SQLite file. Resume asks the user to re-upload if they want them. The accepted **logo** *is* saved (as a data URI) because it's a small flat 2D image and the campaign reuses it across image generations.
- **Not production auth.** No password reset, no email verification, no account deletion flow, no sharing. On Vercel this needs to move to a managed Postgres (Neon / Supabase / Vercel Postgres), blob storage for media, and a real auth provider. That's deliberately out of scope for this localhost demo.

## Evaluation Plan

### Academic Core

**Baseline:** TF-IDF + Logistic Regression
**Main model:** DistilBERT (fine-tuned)

**Metrics:**
- Accuracy
- Macro F1
- Per-class F1 (positive, neutral, negative)
- Confusion matrix

**Analysis:**
- **Neutral/3-star ambiguity:** The 3-star class is inherently noisy — people use it to mean "mediocre," "mixed feelings," and "good product, bad experience." The confusion matrix will show this class bleeding into positive and negative. The report analyzes *why* specific reviews are misclassified (mixed sentiment, conditional praise, sarcasm).
- **Per-category comparison:** If a second category is added, compare model performance across categories. Do beauty reviews behave differently from electronics reviews? Why?
- **Error analysis:** Sample misclassified reviews and categorize failure modes.

**Ablations:**
- **Baseline vs. main model:** TF-IDF + LR vs. DistilBERT — quantify what the deeper model buys you and whether it changes the downstream themes.
- **Generic vs. structured Gemini prompting:** Gemini generating copy with just "write an ad for [product]" (single undifferentiated output) vs. Gemini generating a 3-stage campaign plan with the full structured insight object (distinct objectives, audiences, and creative per stage). Qualitative side-by-side demonstrating the model's downstream value — the comparison is especially pointed because the structured output is a multi-stage plan, not just a better single ad.

**Train/test split caution:** The dataset contains many reviews per product. A naive random split risks placing reviews from the same product in both train and test sets, which can inflate accuracy — the model may learn product-specific language rather than general sentiment patterns. We will use a product-aware split (all reviews for a given product go entirely into train or test, never both) or at minimum document the split strategy clearly and report whether product leakage affects results.

**Honest limitations to report:**
- Star ratings are noisy proxies for sentiment — a 3-star review with mixed sentiment is labeled "neutral" regardless of content.
- TF-IDF is used as a review selection mechanism, not an insight generator. It helps surface representative excerpts, but the terms it produces (e.g., "love," "great") are often too generic to serve as marketing insights on their own. The real value comes from the curated review excerpts passed to Gemini.
- The classifier is trained on star-derived labels. It approximates sentiment at scale but does not discover sentiment beyond what star ratings capture. It should be described as a scalable text-based sentiment approximation, not as a system that corrects or improves upon star ratings.
- The product ranking layer is algorithmic, not a trained recommender. It uses the classifier's output effectively but does not learn ranking preferences from data.
- The system does not have access to business performance data (sales, conversions). Campaign goals are framed in terms of review signal, not revenue metrics.
- Image generation via Nano Banana Pro and video/reel generation via Veo 3.1 Fast are included to demonstrate the end-to-end concept. Quality depends on the generative models and prompts — the pipeline curates the inputs, not the visual output fidelity.
- Video/reel generation via Veo 3.1 is demo-grade and included as a proof of concept.

## Security & Secrets

No API key, token, or secret should ever appear anywhere in this repository — not in source files, notebooks, config files, logs, screenshots, or documentation.

**Local development:** Secrets are stored in a `.env` file at the project root. This file is `.gitignore`d and never committed. A `.env.example` file with placeholder variable names (no real values) is committed to document which keys the project requires.
