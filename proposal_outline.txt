MarketMind AI — Proposal Outline
Review Intelligence for Marketing Generation
Will | MS Applied AI, Lebanese American University


1. PROJECT OVERVIEW

MarketMind AI is a marketing decision-support tool that reads thousands of
product reviews, extracts what customers actually care about, and uses those
insights to generate targeted ad campaigns.

The real-world problem: marketing teams write campaigns based on intuition
or a handful of reviews. They guess at what resonates with buyers. Meanwhile,
thousands of reviews contain clear patterns about what people love, what
frustrates them, and what leaves them indifferent — but nobody reads them
at scale. This project automates that.


2. WHY THIS MATTERS

From an academic perspective: this project demonstrates a full applied AI
pipeline — a trained classification model, an algorithmic product ranking
layer, a structured analysis step, and a generative AI component working
together. Each part has a clear role and is evaluated independently.

From a marketing perspective: the system tells a marketing team which
products to promote for a given campaign goal, shows them exactly what
customers say about those products, and then generates campaign assets
grounded in real customer language — not generic AI copy.


3. DATASET

Source: Amazon Reviews 2023 (McAuley Lab, UC San Diego)
Available publicly on Hugging Face.

Starting category: selected after data exploration based on review quality,
volume, and variety. Likely candidates include All Beauty, Home & Kitchen,
or Electronics.

Sample size: 50,000 reviews for the initial model, 100,000 for the final
version.

Labels: Star ratings (1 to 5) already exist in the dataset. We collapse them
into three classes:
  - Positive (4-5 stars)
  - Neutral (3 stars)
  - Negative (1-2 stars)

Limitation we will state honestly: star ratings are a noisy proxy for
sentiment. A 3-star review that says "great product but terrible shipping"
is genuinely mixed, but gets labeled as neutral. This noise is acknowledged
and analyzed in the evaluation.


4. CORE TASK: SENTIMENT CLASSIFICATION

The trained model reads a product review and predicts whether it is positive,
neutral, or negative. In simple terms, it learns the patterns in language that
distinguish a happy customer from a frustrated one.

This model runs as a batch preprocessing step — it classifies all reviews
in the dataset ahead of time. Each product then gets a precomputed sentiment
profile (percentage of positive, neutral, and negative reviews, plus total
review count) that the rest of the pipeline uses.

We train two models and compare them:

  Baseline — TF-IDF + Logistic Regression:
  A traditional approach. TF-IDF converts review text into numerical features
  based on word frequency. Logistic Regression uses those features to classify
  the review. This is fast, interpretable, and gives us a performance floor
  to beat.

  Main model — DistilBERT (fine-tuned):
  A compact version of BERT, a deep learning language model pretrained on
  large amounts of text. We take the pretrained model from Hugging Face,
  replace its output layer with our 3-class head, and fine-tune it on our
  review data. This model understands context and word order, which helps
  with nuanced reviews that TF-IDF misses.


5. PRODUCT RANKING LAYER

When the user enters campaign constraints (category, campaign goal, target
audience), the system needs to recommend which products to promote. This is
done with algorithmic ranking logic, not a second trained model.

The ranking uses the sentiment profiles from the classifier:

  - If the goal is "highlight strengths," the system ranks products with
    high positive ratios and high review volume — proven winners backed by
    lots of evidence.

  - If the goal is "address objections," it surfaces products with high
    volume but mixed sentiment — products people buy but complain about,
    where better marketing can reframe the narrative.

  - If the goal is "differentiate," it prioritizes products where positive
    reviews mention distinctive attributes — unique strengths the model
    surfaced that competitors lack.

The user sees a ranked list and picks a product to build a campaign around.


6. THEME EXTRACTION

After the user selects a product, we go deeper than just "72% positive."
We ask: what specifically do positive reviewers keep saying?

The method is simple and reproducible:
  1. Take all reviews for the selected product, grouped by predicted sentiment.
  2. Run TF-IDF on each group compared to the others.
     This surfaces the words and phrases that distinguish positive reviews
     from negative ones.
  3. The result:
     - Recurring strengths: terms that appear frequently in positive reviews
       (e.g., "long-lasting," "gentle on skin")
     - Recurring complaints: terms from negative reviews
       (e.g., "broke out," "misleading size")
     - Friction points: terms from neutral reviews
       (e.g., "okay for the price," "nothing special")
  4. For each theme, we pull 2-3 actual review snippets as evidence.

This is not a second trained model. It is a structured analysis step that
uses the classifier's output to produce actionable marketing insights.


7. GEMINI INTEGRATION (AGENTIC LAYER)

The structured insights are passed to Google's Gemini API as a formatted
input object, alongside the campaign brief from the user.

Gemini receives:
  - The product's sentiment profile, strengths, complaints, and friction points
  - The user's chosen campaign goal and target audience

Gemini generates:
  - A campaign angle: a strategic recommendation for how to position the product
  - Ad copy: a headline and body text grounded in real review language
  - A promotional image: generated by Gemini's image capabilities

The key point: without the trained model's output, Gemini writes generic
copy ("Buy our great product!"). With it, Gemini writes copy that addresses
specific buyer concerns and highlights what real customers actually value.
The model decides what to say. Gemini decides how to say it.

The prompt templates used for Gemini are fully documented and disclosed.
Prompt engineering is treated as a deliberate, reproducible contribution.


8. DEMO FLOW

The user interacts with a simple web interface:

  Screen 1 — Campaign Brief:
  Select a product category, a campaign goal, and a target audience tone.
  Click "Find Products."

  Screen 2 — Product Recommendations:
  A ranked list of products suited for the campaign. Each product shows its
  name, sentiment distribution, review count, top strength, and top complaint.
  The user selects a product to build a campaign around.

  Screen 3 — Review Intelligence Card:
  The system shows what it found for the selected product: full sentiment
  distribution, top strengths, top complaints, friction points, and real
  review quotes as evidence. The user sees the reasoning before seeing
  the output.

  Screen 4 — Generated Campaign:
  Campaign angle, ad headline, body copy, and a promotional image.
  A short note explains which review insights drove the creative decisions.


9. EVALUATION AND ACADEMIC RIGOR

What we evaluate:
  - Accuracy: overall proportion of correct predictions
  - Macro F1: average F1 score across all three classes, treating each equally
  - Per-class F1: separate F1 for positive, neutral, and negative
  - Confusion matrix: a table showing where the model gets confused

Why the neutral class is interesting:
  The 3-star / neutral class will be the hardest to classify. People use
  3 stars to mean very different things — "it's fine," "mixed feelings,"
  "good product but bad experience." The confusion matrix will show this
  class bleeding into both positive and negative. The report analyzes why
  specific reviews are misclassified, with concrete examples.

Ablations and comparisons:
  - Baseline vs. main model: TF-IDF + Logistic Regression vs. DistilBERT.
    We quantify exactly how much the deeper model improves performance and
    whether it changes the downstream themes.
  - Generic vs. structured Gemini prompting: We compare Gemini's output
    when given just "write an ad for [product]" versus when given the full
    structured insight object from our pipeline. This is a qualitative
    side-by-side that demonstrates the trained model's value in the overall
    system.


10. LIMITATIONS (STATED HONESTLY)

  - Star ratings are noisy labels. A review with mixed sentiment gets a
    single star rating that may not reflect the text.
  - Theme extraction uses TF-IDF on grouped reviews. It produces recurring
    terms, not deep aspect-level understanding. We call the outputs
    "recurring strengths and complaints," not "emotional triggers."
  - The product ranking layer is algorithmic, not a trained recommender.
    It uses the classifier's output effectively but does not learn ranking
    preferences from data.
  - The system has no access to business data like sales or conversions.
    Campaign goals are framed around review signals, not revenue metrics.
  - Gemini's image generation quality varies. The generated images support
    the demo but are not production-grade marketing assets.


11. FEASIBILITY

  - Dataset is public, pre-labeled, and well-documented.
  - The baseline model trains in under 2 minutes.
  - DistilBERT fine-tunes in 20-90 minutes on a free Google Colab GPU.
  - The full model development process fits into 5-7 working sessions
    spread across 2-3 weeks.
  - No paid compute, no proprietary data, no complex infrastructure.
  - The project is scoped to be completable within course constraints
    while still demonstrating a full applied AI pipeline from trained
    model to working demo.
